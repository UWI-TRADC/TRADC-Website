Hackathon Ideas

  1. Decentralized Blogging Platform: Similar to Medium or WordPress, but with content stored on the blockchain, ensuring censorship resistance and content ownership.
  2. Crypto Wallet Tracker: A platform similar to Blockfolio or Delta, where users can track their cryptocurrency holdings and transactions.
  3. Decentralized Job Board: A platform like LinkedIn or Indeed, but focused on blockchain and crypto jobs, with smart contracts handling job agreements and payments.
  4. Blockchain-based Event Ticketing: A system similar to Eventbrite or Ticketmaster, where tickets are issued as NFTs, ensuring authenticity and preventing fraud.
  5. Decentralized Music Streaming: A platform like Spotify or SoundCloud, where artists can upload their music and receive payments directly through crypto.
  6. Crypto-based Loyalty Rewards: A system similar to loyalty reward programs, but rewards are given in cryptocurrency or tokens.
  7. Decentralized E-Learning Platform: A platform like Udemy or Coursera, where educators can upload courses and receive payments in cryptocurrency.
  8. Blockchain-based Real Estate Listing: A platform similar to Zillow or Realtor.com, where property listings are stored on the blockchain, ensuring transparency and authenticity.
  9. Decentralized Ride-Sharing: A platform like Uber or Lyft, but operates on the blockchain, ensuring transparent ride agreements and payments.
  10. Crypto Subscription Service: A platform similar to Patreon, where content creators can receive recurring payments in cryptocurrency from their supporters.
  11. Blockchain-based Auction House: A platform like eBay, but with auctions handled by smart contracts to ensure fairness and transparency.
  12. Decentralized File Storage: A platform similar to Dropbox or Google Drive, but where files are stored across a decentralized network.
  13. Crypto-based Affiliate Marketing: A system where affiliates are rewarded in cryptocurrency for referring customers.
  14. Decentralized News Platform: A platform like Reddit or Hacker News, where news is curated and voted on by the community, and content is stored on the blockchain.
  15. Blockchain-based Travel Booking: A platform like Airbnb or Booking.com, where listings and bookings are stored on the blockchain, ensuring transparency.
  16. Decentralized Social Networking: A platform similar to Facebook or Twitter, but where user data is stored on the blockchain, ensuring privacy and data ownership.
  17. Crypto-based Crowdsourced Reviews: A platform like Yelp or TripAdvisor, where reviews are stored on the blockchain, ensuring authenticity.
  18. Blockchain-based Fitness Tracking: A platform like Fitbit or MyFitnessPal, where fitness data is stored on the blockchain, ensuring data ownership and privacy.
  19. Decentralized E-commerce Marketplace: A platform like Amazon or Etsy, but operates on the blockchain, ensuring transparent sales agreements and payments.
  20. Crypto-based Fundraising for Non-profits: A platform similar to GoFundMe, but for nonprofits to raise funds in cryptocurrency.

Rules

  • All projects must use proper version control (for example: Github).

    Any commits of large files without proper history will be default assumed to be unqualified unless proven otherwise.

  • No teams. All submissions are expected to be individual work.

    Any submissions found to be otherwise will be assumed unqualified.

Using open-source libraries and boilerplates

  • Using open-source libraries and boilerplates is encouraged.
  • In order to keep the event fair to all participants, we require hackers to start their projects when the event begins so that no individual has a head-start.
  • You are, however, welcome to use any open source libraries, starter-kits/boilerplates, or design a spec for your hack.
  • You will be asked to share the source code (GitHub Repo, Figma, etc.) and show that work for the event.

Attending a Judging Session

Each team has 7 minutes in total (4 minutes for the demo and 3 minutes for Q&A with the judges)...

Some Questions to Expect:

  • Explain the motivation behind your project
  • What type of tools did you use, and why?
  • What was your inspiration?

Judging Criteria

Each project will be ranked by our judges on the following 5 categories:...

  • Technicality: What is the complexity of the problem being addressed, or their approach to solving it?
  • Originality: Is the project creating a unique/creative solution to an existing problem?
  • Practicality: How complete/functional is the project? Is it ready to be used by its intended audience?
  • Usability (UI/UX/DX): Is the project easy to use? Has the team made a good effort in removing friction for the user?
  • WOW Factor: Catch-all for other factors the previous categories may have missed.

Tips for a Good Demo Video

To record the video you can use tools like Loom, Zoom screen-share, or QuickTime Player...

  • Minimum Video Length: 2 minutes
  • Maximum Video Length: 4 minutes
  • Important Note: DO NOT speed up your video to fit the time requirement. If judges can't understand what you're presenting, they won't be able to give effective feedback.

How to Present Your Project

  • Speak Slowly and Clearly: Ensure that your speech is clear and easy to understand.
  • Do a Few Practice Takes: Practice your presentation to improve your delivery and avoid filler sounds like "uhm."
  • Avoid Echo-ey Sound: Use a good microphone to avoid echo-ey sound. Consider trying a different room with better acoustics, perhaps even a closet.
  • Keep Introductions and Project Summary Short: Limit introductions and project summaries to less than 20 seconds. Avoid spending too much time on backstory.
  • Crop Out Needless Waiting Time: Remove unnecessary waiting time, such as Metamask TX confirmations, to keep the video focused.
  • Be Prepared for Form Filling or Switching Between Views: Have forms or views ready in multiple tabs to ensure a smooth transition during the demo.
  • Use Slides to Summarize Key Information: Utilize slides to present key information in a clear and organized manner.
  • Limit Bullet Points on Slides: Don't use more than 4 bullet points per slide to maintain clarity and avoid overwhelming the audience.
